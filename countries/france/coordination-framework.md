# France: Palliative Care Volunteer Coordination

## National Framework

France's volunteer coordination in palliative care is defined by the concept of **Accompagnement** and is governed by:

- **Plan national de développement des soins palliatifs et de l'accompagnement** (National plan for palliative care development)
- **Loi n°2016-87 (Claeys-Leonetti)**: Law creating new rights for end-of-life patients
- **SFAP (Société Française d'Accompagnement et de soins Palliatifs)**: National professional society
- **HAS (Haute Autorité de Santé)**: National health authority — quality standards

### Governance Structure
- **Ministry of Health**: Sets national strategy and funding
- **Regional health agencies (ARS)**: Agences Régionales de Santé — implement and fund regional programs
- **SFAP**: Professional society setting practice guidelines
- **Local associations**: Over 300 accredited accompaniment associations across France

### Legal Context
- **Loi n°2016-87 (Claeys-Leonetti, 2016)**: Created right to deep sedation; strengthened accompaniment rights
- **Loi n°2005-370 (Leonetti, 2005)**: First law on end-of-life rights and limitations on euthanasia
- **Code de la santé publique**: Public health code provisions for palliative care
- **Accreditation requirement**: Volunteer accompaniment associations must be accredited by ARS
- **Article L. 1110-11**: Recognizes the role of volunteers in palliative care accompaniment

## Volunteer Operational Model

### The French Accompagnement Model

The French model is built around **Accompagnement** — a philosophy of being present and accompanying, not intervening:

1. **Recruitment**: Through accredited local associations; personal interviews; motivation assessment
2. **Training**: 20–30 hours, covering:
   - Accompagnement philosophy and ethics
   - Communication and listening skills
   - Grief and bereavement (deuil)
   - Teamwork with healthcare professionals
   - Self-care and personal reflection
   - Legal framework (Leonetti law)
3. **Deployment**: Volunteers work in hospitals, palliative care units, and home settings
4. **Supervision**: Regular group supervision (groupe de parole); individual support
5. **Recognition**: National Journée du Souvenir; association events; ongoing training

### Role Categories

| Role | Activities |
|------|------------|
| Accompagnateur (accompaniment volunteer) | Being present, listening, emotional support |
| Accompagnateur à domicile (home accompaniment) | Home visits, practical support, family support |
| Bereavement support | Deuil accompaniment, support groups |
| Administrative | Association governance, fundraising, communication |

### Distinctive Features
- **Accreditation system**: Volunteer associations must be formally accredited by regional health agencies
- **Accompagnement philosophy**: Distinct from care — volunteers accompany, not care for
- **Legal recognition**: French law explicitly recognizes and protects the role of accompaniment volunteers
- **Groupe de parole**: Mandatory group supervision/debriefing sessions
- **Strong hospital integration**: Volunteers formally embedded in hospital palliative care teams

## Quality Standards

- HAS quality standards for palliative care
- SFAP guidelines for volunteer accompaniment
- ARS accreditation requirements for associations
- National palliative care program indicators

## Key Challenges

1. **Access inequality**: Significant disparities between regions (Paris vs. rural areas)
2. **Association governance**: Small associations struggle with management and sustainability
3. **Professional-volunteer boundary**: Tensions between "accompagnement" and clinical care roles
4. **Recruitment diversity**: Volunteer base predominantly older, middle-class
5. **Funding**: ARS funding varies; some associations rely heavily on donations
