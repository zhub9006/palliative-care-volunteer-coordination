# Canada: Palliative Care Volunteer Coordination

## National Framework

Canada has one of the most developed volunteer coordination models in the world, guided by the **Canadian Hospice Palliative Care Association (CHPCA)**. The foundational document is **"A Model to Guide Hospice Palliative Care: Based on National Principles and Norms of Practice"** (2002, updated 2013), supplemented by a dedicated **Volunteer Model** document.

### Governance Structure
- **Federal**: Health Canada provides policy direction; no single federal palliative care act
- **Provincial/Territorial**: Each province has its own health legislation and funding model for palliative care
- **CHPCA**: National body that sets norms of practice, including volunteer standards
- **CCHSA**: Canadian Council on Health Services Accreditation — accredits hospice programs

### Legal Context
- No specific federal legislation mandating volunteer coordination
- Provincial health acts govern care delivery
- Volunteer protection varies by province (e.g., Ontario's Volunteer Protection Act)
- Accreditation standards require structured volunteer programs

## Volunteer Operational Model

### The CHPCA Volunteer Model

The CHPCA Volunteer Model defines volunteers as **vital members of the caring team**, not adjuncts. Key features:

1. **Recruitment**: Open recruitment with screening interviews, reference checks, and police checks
2. **Training**: 30–40 hours of standardized training covering:
   - Philosophy of hospice palliative care
   - Communication skills
   - Grief and bereavement
   - Boundaries and self-care
   - Cultural sensitivity
3. **Deployment**: Volunteers assigned based on patient/family needs, matched by VSM
4. **Supervision**: Regular check-ins with Volunteer Services Manager; peer support groups
5. **Recognition**: Formal recognition events; ongoing education opportunities

### Role Definition

| Role Category | Activities |
|--------------|------------|
| Direct Patient Support | Companionship, active listening, life review, practical assistance |
| Family Support | Respite care, bereavement follow-up, practical help |
| Administrative | Office support, fundraising, event coordination |
| Professional/Specialized | Music therapy, pet therapy, spiritual care (with additional training) |

### Scope of Practice Boundaries
- Volunteers do NOT: administer medications, provide medical advice, perform personal care (in most settings), make clinical assessments
- Volunteers DO: provide emotional support, companionship, practical assistance, advocacy, and bereavement support

## Quality Standards

- CHPCA Norms of Practice apply to all volunteer programs
- Accreditation through CCHSA requires evidence of volunteer program governance
- Regular program evaluation using standardized tools
- Volunteer satisfaction surveys and outcome tracking

## Key Challenges

1. Post-COVID volunteer attrition and difficulty recruiting
2. Inconsistent training standards across provinces
3. Rural/urban disparities in volunteer availability
4. Integration of volunteers into interdisciplinary care teams
