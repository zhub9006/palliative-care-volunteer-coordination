# Singapore: Palliative Care Volunteer Coordination

## National Framework

Singapore has a well-organized, government-directed approach to palliative care volunteer coordination:

- **2023 National Strategy for Palliative Care** — Latest comprehensive national strategy
- **Ministry of Health (MOH)** — Policy direction, regulation, and funding
- **Singapore Hospice Council (SHC)** — National coordinating body for hospice providers
- **National Guidelines for Palliative Care** (Revised Edition, 2015) — SHC standards

### Governance Structure
- **MOH**: Central policy and funding authority
- **Regional health systems**: Coordinate palliative care across different care settings
- **SHC**: Coordinates hospice providers; sets standards
- **Individual hospice organizations**: Manage their own volunteer programs

### Legal Context
- **MOH regulations**: Govern hospice and palliative care service delivery
- **Volunteer protection**: Limited specific legislation
- **Healthcare Services Act (2019)**: Regulates healthcare service providers
- **Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA)**: Applies to volunteer access to patient information

## Volunteer Operational Model

### The Singapore Integrated Model

Singapore's model is **formal and integrated** within the national healthcare system:

1. **Recruitment**: Through hospice organizations, community outreach, SHC
2. **Training**: 20–30 hours, covering:
   - Palliative care philosophy and principles
   - Communication and listening skills
   - Grief and bereavement
   - Cultural sensitivity (multi-ethnic context)
   - Self-care and resilience
   - Singapore's healthcare system context
3. **Deployment**: Volunteers in hospices, hospitals, and community/home settings
4. **Supervision**: Dedicated volunteer coordinators in each hospice; regular check-ins
5. **Recognition**: National Volunteer & Philanthropy Centre awards; hospice events

### Role Categories

| Role | Activities |
|------|------------|
| Befriender | Companionship, emotional support, conversation |
| Home care volunteer | Home visits, practical assistance, caregiver respite |
| Day hospice volunteer | Activity facilitation, social engagement, meal support |
| Bereavement support | Grief support groups, follow-up calls |
| Administrative | Fundraising, events, community awareness |

### Distinctive Features
- **Multi-ethnic context**: Training must address Chinese, Malay, Indian, and other cultural perspectives
- **Government integration**: Strong alignment between MOH strategy and volunteer programs
- **Regional health system partnerships**: Coordination between hospices and regional health systems
- **Compact geography**: Easier coordination compared to larger countries
- **2023 strategy emphasis**: Stronger partnerships between regional health systems and community care providers

## Quality Standards

- SHC National Guidelines for Palliative Care
- MOH service standards
- National Strategy quality indicators
- Individual hospice quality frameworks

## Key Challenges

1. **Aging population**: Growing demand outpacing volunteer supply
2. **Cultural barriers**: Death and dying remain taboo in some communities
3. **Volunteer recruitment**: Competition with other volunteering opportunities
4. **Sustainability**: Funding models under pressure
